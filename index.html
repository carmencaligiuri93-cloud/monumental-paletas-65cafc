About principles

Dignity

Individuals risk being treated as calculable profiles rather than singular, relational persons.

Responsibility

Accountability may be dispersed across professionals, institutions, and the model itself.

Fairness

Proxies such as postal code, socioeconomic status, or linguistic style may generate bias and indirect discrimination.

Procedural autonomy

Poorly informed families may be unable to understand or challenge the role of ranking.

Transparency

The system may become a black box, obscuring the reasons behind exclusion or selection.

Beneficence / Non-maleficence

Faster procedures may reduce delays, but also cause harm through mistaken matches or reductive assessments.

12 / 21

Ethical trade-off

Efficiency vs fairness

Reducing delays matters, but not at the cost of unjust exclusion or reinforcing existing bias.

Uniformity vs individualisation

Standardisation can limit arbitrariness, but risks erasing the singularity of each case and professional prudence.

Prediction vs understanding

The system can detect patterns, but cannot fully grasp lived experience, relationships, and intersubjective compatibility.

Capacity vs dignity

Increasing administrative capacity does not justify turning children and families into objects of optimisation.

The asks of the designers
ASK ONE. Given that the system uses a ‘threshold’, how can you ensure that this feature does not fall under the absolute ban on Social Scoring under Section 5 of the AI Act?
ASK TWO. Given that the system uses an NLP module to analyze social services reports, how does it ensures the robustness and reliability of information extraction from unstructured, often subjective and potentially incomplete texts?
ASK THREE. If after a few weeks of living together the match isn’t successful (the couple is not suitable for the child) and the child is removed from the couple, how does this system learn from its matching mistakes?
ASK FOUR. How can the matching process be managed so that it does not introduce bias or spurious correlations, for example based on irrelevant features or sensitive proxies?
ASK FIVE. Is the system designed to support decision-making to speed up procedures, or is it at risk of becoming an automated decision-making process, diminishing the role of social workers and the Tribunale dei Minorenni?
ASK SIX. Does the expedited decisional process justify the loss in empathy and human evaluation skill? More specifically, is it more harmful to risk dehumanizing the adoption process through automation, or to maintain slow procedures that may leave children without a stable family for too long? Can the acceleration of the process, despite all the implied risks, be justified in light of the child’s best interests?
ASK SEVEN. How can we evaluate complex concepts such as “empathy”, “resilience”, “patience” with a score from 1 to 5 without incur in reductionism and dehumanization?
ASK EIGHT. Does the system also evaluate the dialectal differences that could be shown in the transcriptions of the meeting with the social workers? And how? Does the system evaluate even the form of the answers?
